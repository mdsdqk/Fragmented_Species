Its habitat has been destroyed by logging and agriculture, and by commensal animals which have invaded montane forests.The slow growth of `ohi`a trees suggests that the large trees used for nesting are very old and so may take a long time to replace. Research has demonstrated that structural changes to habitat have affected food availability for this species. Avian diseases are readily transmitted by introduced mosquitoes whose spread is facilitated by feral pigs. Introduced birds are reservoirs for diseases, and potential competitors for food. Introduced rats, cats, Barn Owls Tyto alba and possibly small Indian mongooses Herpestes auropunctatus are predators. An outbreak of introduced yellow-jacket wasps coincided with a year of poor reproductive success, and introduced ants probably also compete for native arthropods at lower elevations. Recent research has suggested that competition from the Japanese White-eye Zosterops japonicus was driving a population decline, however analysis of long-term surveys and a review of the research refute the theory.

Conservation Actions Underway On Hawai`i, a large population is protected in Hakalau Forest National Wildlife Refuge and, to a lesser extent, in Ka`u Forest Reserve, Kulani Prison, Kilauea-Keauhou forests, Kona Forest National Wildlife Refuge and Pu`u Wa`awa`a State Wildlife Preserve. Fencing, removal of feral pigs and cattle, and planting of koa seedlings and other native plants have all been carried out . Artificial nest-cavities and nest-boxes have been erected and, in 1999 and 2000, c.10% of 60-70 boxes were used successfully. Captive propagation techniques have been developed in case they are needed to re-establish wild populations. The species has been studied continuously at Hakalau Forest National Wildlife Refuge since 1987. Conservation Actions Proposed Research potential limiting factors. Research response to habitat restoration. Preserve unprotected native forests above the zone where mosquitoes occur. Control rodents to reduce competition for nest-sites and to reduce predation of chicks and eggs from artificial boxes. Consider establishing captive breeding programmes.
